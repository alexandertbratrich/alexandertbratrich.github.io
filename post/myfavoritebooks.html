My Favorite Books

2020-12-15 by Alexander T. Bratrich 12 min read

When I was younger I didn't read much, but nowadays I believe I'm an avid reader, although I know I could read even more throughout the years. By popular demand (aka, at least one person asked), here's my list of all books I've ever read.

Most comments are kept extremely neutral to avoid spoilers and wrong memories. I ranked them by my personal arbitrary gut-reaction, in a totally not scientific biased way. For a book to have only a rating of 1 or 2 doesn't mean it's bad (otherwise I wouldn't have listed it at all), it probably just means it's more a non-fiction book that might still be interesting or important, but wouldn't re-read every year with enjoyment. My ›5-emoji-rating‹ can't be really compared to the usual 5-star-Amazon rating. I'm not a harsh critic. If I like a series or an author, I might like all books in that area. And in general, my rating shouldn't be taken too seriously.

Each entry also includes the date I've read them. The list of this year do not include any re-reads of previous books (like Scott Pilgrim for example).

My 2020 Read

The Fault in Our Stars by John Green

Without a doubt John Green's best work, with a great movie adaptation. Made me cry and didn't get out of my head for weeks.
5/5 2020

Will Grayson, Will Grayson by John Green and David Levithan

I like the concept of two authors taking turns writing chapters.
5/5 2020

Turtles All the Way Down by John Green

It's John Green, so of course I would enjoy it.
5/5 2020

Let It Snow by Maureen Johnson, John Green and Lauren Myracle

Before I read it, I thought it would be some kind of traditional christmas story (although it says ›Three Holiday Romances‹ on the cover). Turns out, it was more than that. Mrs. Johnson's story I enjoyed the most out the three; John Green's story was a bit predictable compared to his other works (but still good; I mean, they all were a bit, but I think that's the point of romances especially short ones). Overall, I enjoyed all three stories. I like the concept of three separate stories by different authors in the same setting.
4/5 2020

Looking for Alaska by John Green

Still in the middle of reading this one, while I finished this years list. But I think it's obvious that I like John Green.
4/5 2020

No Plot? No Problem! by Chris Baty

Written by the guy who founded NaNoWriMo, this book is almost as old as the NaNoWriMo itself. So literally all the information in this book is available online nowadays. Still a fun read to put you in the right mindset.
3/5 2020

The Dip by Seth Godin

To be honest, it's not a good book. It feels like Mr. Godin just read about the already known concept of the Plateau, renamed it and quickly punched out a book to make a quick buck, even having the audacity to say that ›Short books are hard to write, but you made me do it.‹ Yeah, nah. It's not really going very deep into real-life examples, giving new insights or real concrete tips, just repeating the same shallow definition over and over. That being said, the message behind it is correct and important, but you can read all of it online in a fraction of the time, with even more insight.
1/5 2020

A book that's soon a hundred years old. An interesting read for any introvert like me.
