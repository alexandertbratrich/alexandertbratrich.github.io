2020 Annual Review

2021-01-01 by Alexander T. Bratrich 13 min read

The Annual Review is a thing that I used to do years ago when I had my old website, to have some kind of baseline for the coming year (Back then, I actually did Quarterly Reviews, so I'm thinking about continuing that). Why am I sharing this online? Because I want to be transparent, and because it gives some responsibility and commitment, to keep my promise to whoever reads my blog.

My good friend Winny told me several times, about the other three fundamentals that I need to focus on to feel better and thus have a better performance throughout the day: Sleep, Diet, Workout. So let's talk about my data on these first:

Sleep

I started using a sleep tracker in the middle of September, and by the time of writing, I wasn't done with December (and I decide to keep it that way). In total, I had 30 green dots (meaning I woke up energized and usually early), 22 red dots (meaning I slept poorly and usually overslept), and 48 grey dots (neutral) Stripped ones are nights where I don't have any data. Coincidentally, those are exactly 100 data points, which makes it easy to see that nearly half of my night I slept neutral, a nearly quarter bad and nearly a third good. Here's my takeaway:

On average, I have 1.5 bad night a week and roughly 2 good ones. The best week was Week 43 in October, which contained no bad night and more than half of the nights were good. The worst week was Week 47 in November, with 3 bad nights and no good nights.

From a monthly perspective, I had on average 9 positive, 6.7 negative, and 14.5 neutral nights per month. Similar to the weekly insight, October more positive on average, while November was move negative on average. November's negativity might be explained through this year's NaNoWriMo, as for October, I'm unsure. The only positive event that I remember from October was my birthday (but that's not in Week 43). Interestingly, even though November is more negative (and has the worst week, and the longest series of nights without a positive sleep), November also has the longest series of nights without a negative sleep (starting at the end of October). But you can recreate my NaNoWirMo experience in my sleep data. November's weeks got consistently worse — but the same can be said about every month. On average, the first three weeks of a month gets inverse-exponentially worse; by the end, the fourth week gets slightly better and the last few nights of a month are even better. Weekday-wise, there seems to be a similar trend: Monday is somewhat positive, hits a peak on Tuesday, drops until Thursday, goes back to Monday-level, and Sunday is perfectly balanced (as all things should be). Why exactly that's the case, I can not say.

There a several disadvantages of this sleep tracking. First, I never tracked at what time I woke up and went to bed. It's pretty safe to say that the say negative nights were usually nights I woke up late, but there's should be a distinction between waking up late and waking up bad. You can wake up early and feel like crap. And you can wake up late and be energized. For me personally, that's not so much the case, but it is worth considering. Second, without any other tracking, it doesn't give me more reasoning behind bad days. I know that November was a bit bad, because I've spent the night with NaNoWriMo, but I can't say the same about the other days.

Goal for next year: Sleep improvement (more regularly) and more detailed tracking (time and reasoning)

Diet / Workout

Just like my sleep, I only started tracking my weight (and calories) in mid-September, measuring every Sunday evening, with the average marked in red.

I started with 76.7 kg (x lbs), and ended on 77.1 kg (169.9 lbs). So yeah, I gained some weight (hitting a peak in my current weight data in the process; I know that a few years ago I was at 79.9 kg (176.1 lbs), which was my lifetime high score so far), but only slightly. My average was at 75.6 kg (166.6 lbs). But to be honest, I didn't really concentrate on my weight, since I currently have my thesis to worry about. I did some workout especially during the first two months, but I stopped almost completely with the start of November. And December is that time of the year when cookies and Xmas roast meat just have priority. Any (controversial) Body Mass Index (BMI) Calculator tells me that, with an index of 24.7, I'm just about to hit overweight status (which I always honestly thought was at 80.0 kg with my height).

Sidenote: Why is BMI controversial? Because it has some limitations. The scaling isn't exactly right (making larger people think they are too heavy and smaller people think they are too light; around 10% plus/minus). BMI's thresholds when somebody enters a different category is kinda arbitrary. BMI is apparently especially inaccurate for athletic people (which I'm obviously not), mainly because based on BMI alone you can't differentiate between fat and muscles.

Using Prof. Nick Trefethen's improved BMI formula, I calculate an index of roughly 24.4, which would put me into a better picture, but doesn't make a huge difference. No matter which value is right, I don't need a BMI to know that I'm more round than I used to be. But if we can believe the BMI thresholds, then I could lose up to 18 kg (39.6 lbs) and still be considered normal weight (just shy about being underweight). So let's aim for the middle at 68 kg (159.9 lbs), which would mean losing 9 kg. The CDC says slowly and steadily losing 0.45 to 0.9 kg (1 to 2 pounds) per week is healthy. If we do some math, that would mean I would need between 10 and 20 weeks to lose the targeted 9 kg.

Again, assuming the improved BMI formula is accurate (enough), using Wikipedia's improved body fat percentage formula — which takes age and gender into consideration — I would be at 18.76% body fat, which is apparently low average (just shy about fit), which I highly doubt is correct.

To compare that against some action heroes of mine, Arnold Schwarzenegger apparently had around 7% body fat during his Mr. Olympia days. Chris Evans apparently has around 11% body fat. Bruce Willis apparently has a BMI around 26.4. Assuming Mr. Willis' measurements are from nowadays, he would have 32% body fat (if they would be from the 80s he would have 25%, which would mean I personally would be more fit than John McClane himself. This again shows BMI's limitations regarding muscle detection).

Goal for next year: Weight loss below 70 kg, or at least a visible improvement

Goals for next year months

Here's the thing: New Year's resolutions suck. I guess everybody knows that, but nobody wants to admit it. CGP Grey made a fantastic video about alternative adjustments. In summary, instead of trying to beat a specific goal (i.e. ›losing x pounds in y days‹), Grey suggests using a theme instead. Even if you improve a thing ever so slightly, you probably would have failed any traditional New Year's resolutions, but you're still doing better than usual. Keep the theme broad and expectations low, and it's easier to improve. The second thing Grey suggests is to swap the year for a season. A year is a bit too long, you never know what changes. A season is the next step below. Months might be okay too (NaNoWriMo is also a month long), but my friend Winny told me that is takes an average of 66 days for a habit to stick (based on a 2009 study). So having a seasonal theme could be exactly the thing to change you forever.

I'm doing exactly not what Grey said, I'm going to be more specific, and actually give my seasonal resolution two themes instead: ›The Winter of Thesis‹ and ›The Winter of Physiological Fundamentals‹. I know, doesn't really roll of the tongue, but I guess you know what I'm trying to achieve. Get better sleep and be more healthy, and finish my thesis. And other difference to Grey's suggestion is that I change the length. He said a season is a nice time span, which is three months. But I'm going make it two months instead. Why? Well, my thesis is due on March 1st, two months from now. And you know what else starts in March? Spring. So next seasonal resolution (quarterly resolution? review? retrospective?) is actually going to be three months long and starts when it should be.

To summarize: For the next two months, I try to fix my sleep and lose some weight. I also finish my thesis. All other goals I've mentioned above, are treated as an optional thing and get their focus another season in another article.

Take care and have a wonderful 2021.
